This is such a real and interesting split. The "non-math person" identity often forms around a very specific thing — arithmetic speed, classroom performance, test anxiety, whatever got marked as "math" by school — and then just... sticks, even as the actual capability keeps developing in different directions.

Matrix algebra in code is a different cognitive mode than mental arithmetic. You're manipulating structure and relationships — what shape does this need to be, what operation transforms it meaningfully, does this dimensionality make sense — which is much closer to spatial/logical reasoning than to the numerical computation school tested you on. The fact that it works is evidence that you were never actually bad at math in any deep sense. You were bad at a specific performance format.

The classroom thing is similar. You process best through structured text and databases, you have dyslexia, you have a genuinely cross-disciplinary mind that doesn't stay in a lane — none of that fits the "sit through lecture, regurgitate on exam" format particularly well. That format is optimized to evaluate a very narrow slice of cognition, and it penalizes exactly the kind of lateral, framework-bridging thinking you actually do.

The vibecoding angle is interesting too — you can offload the syntactic friction of "write the exact numpy call" while keeping full ownership of the conceptual decisions. What should this operation do, what structure does the result need to have. That's the part that actually requires mathematical understanding, and you're clearly doing it.

The "non-math person" story is just not load-bearing anymore at this point in your trajectory. Might be worth letting it go.

That's a sharp observation. Let me push on it a bit.

The embedding space constraint you're pointing at is real — the geometry of dense vectors handles graded similarity well but struggles with the full relational structure of many-to-many mappings because every entity gets one point (or one region), and distance/direction has to do all the semantic work. You can approximate "king - man + woman = queen" style relations, but that's still essentially a one-to-one asymmetric transformation applied uniformly. When a relation is genuinely many-to-many — one concept participating in multiple incompatible relational roles simultaneously — the geometry starts lying to you or averaging things it shouldn't.

A few directions that handle this better:

Hypergraph / tensor models — relations as hyperedges rather than pairwise edges. A tensor decomposition (CP, Tucker) can represent n-ary relations without forcing them through a binary bottleneck. This is actually what's happening under the hood in some knowledge graph completion work.

Type-theoretic / categorial approaches — if you represent entities not as points but as types with ports, many-to-many structure becomes natural. Functorial semantics, categorical compositional distributional semantics (DisCoCat and descendants). The relational structure is in the morphisms, not the objects.

RDF-star / property graphs — which you already think in — are essentially a syntactic acknowledgment of this problem. Reification lets a relation itself be an argument to another relation, which is one way to escape the flatness.

The interesting question for your isosymbolic framework specifically: do the FOL operations you've found in embedding space implicitly encode many-to-many relations through composition of asymmetric ones, or is that genuinely outside what the geometry can represent even with operations stacked? Because if it's the latter, that's a real boundary condition on where the isosymbolic zone breaks down — not just a sparsity/collision problem but a structural one.

The honest answer is that many-to-many relations might just be outside the isosymbolic zone by definition, not a failure mode but a boundary condition. The geometry of embedding space is fundamentally a metric space over points — and a metric space can only natively express relations that reduce to pairwise distance. Many-to-many structure requires something more like a set membership primitive, which isn't metric.

So you might have three tiers of representational cost:

  • Embedding space — handles asymmetric one-to-one well, approximates some transitive ones, many-to-many is structurally excluded
  • Graph / RDF — handles many-to-many natively but at traversal cost, property graphs add another layer of overhead on top
  • Type-theoretic / hypergraph — full expressive power but you're basically leaving the "cheap inference" regime entirely

The question worth sitting with is whether many-to-many relations in language are actually primitive or whether they're composed. Like — is "ingredient of" many-to-many, or is it a conjunction of many asymmetric one-to-one "ingredient-of-instance" relations that just look many-to-many at the schema level? If the latter, your embedding operations might be able to handle them through decomposition rather than needing a new representational substrate.

The WordPiece diacritic finding is almost an example of this going wrong in the other direction — the tokenizer is implicitly assuming a many-to-one collapse is safe when it isn't, which is why the geometry breaks.

Yes — mathematics is saturated with many-to-many relations. Functors between categories, correspondences in algebraic geometry, multivalued functions, sheaves, spans and cospans. The whole field of categorical logic is essentially a sustained attempt to handle structure that resists flat directional encoding.

The "flat directional ontology" framing is really precise. Natural language does this too — it's why analogy and metaphor work at all, you're always projecting a richer relational structure onto a directed similarity judgment. The language flattens it and you reconstruct. Embedding spaces just make that flattening explicit and lossy.

The "something between" is interesting. What you seem to want is:

  • Cheaper than a full knowledge graph (no explicit edge materialization, no traversal overhead)
  • But capable of representing membership/participation structure that metric space can't

One thing that might be in that zone: sparse relational structure on top of dense embeddings. Not a full graph, but something like a lightweight incidence structure — which entities co-participate in which relations, without fully reifying the relations as nodes. This is closer to how factor graphs work. You get many-to-many participation without full graph overhead.

The other candidate is contextualized embeddings as implicit many-to-many — the same token has different vectors in different contexts, which is sort of a hack that approximates participation in multiple relational structures without representing them explicitly. Transformers are doing something like this but nobody's really formalized what relational structure they're actually capturing.

14:46

Yes, exactly. And that's a deep problem not a tuning problem — hyperbolic space requires the tree to be real. The curvature is doing work precisely because hierarchy is assumed to be the ground truth structure. So when your data has genuine ambiguity, lateral relations, things that belong to multiple branches, the geometry doesn't gracefully degrade, it catastrophically misrepresents. You get confident wrong answers instead of uncertain right ones.

It's the Deleuzian criticism of arborescent thought made into a mathematical constraint. The tree model isn't just inconvenient, it actively suppresses rhizomatic structure by punishing it metrically.

And natural language and mathematics both have irreducibly rhizomatic regions. A concept that genuinely participates in multiple incompatible hierarchies isn't a data quality problem, it's a feature of the domain. Hyperbolic embeddings treat it as noise.

So the failure mode is almost the inverse of your WordPiece finding — diacritics cause collapse (things that should be distinct become identical), hyperbolic embeddings cause forced separation along a rigid branching structure (things with genuine lateral relations get assigned to exactly one branch and the others are lossy approximations or errors).

Both are cases where the representational substrate is making ontological commitments the data doesn't actually support.

The "something between" you're looking for might need to be commitment-light as a core design principle. Not just cheaper than a knowledge graph but deliberately less opinionated about what kind of structure the relations have. Which is almost the opposite of hyperbolic's design philosophy.

Oh that's genuinely interesting. You're describing something like a navigable ontological manifold where the small-world property gives you cheap traversal across abstraction levels without committing to a fixed tree structure.

HNSW works because it builds a hierarchy of graphs with decreasing connectivity — upper layers are long-range coarse navigation, lower layers are dense local search. The hierarchy is emergent from the data not imposed on it. That's the key difference from hyperbolic space where the hierarchy is baked into the geometry as a prior.

If you train an explicit "ontological height" dimension — not derived from curvature but learned as a feature — then something HNSW-like could navigate it without the arborescent commitment. You ascend to find the right abstraction level, descend to find specific instances, but the lateral relations at any given height are still metric and can be rhizomatic. A concept can have meaningful neighbors at its height without those neighbors being siblings in a fixed tree.

The small-world property is doing real work here. Small-world networks have high clustering and short path lengths — which means local ontological neighborhoods are dense and coherent, but you can still reach distant concepts cheaply by ascending and re-descending. That's actually a better model of how human conceptual navigation seems to work than either flat embeddings or strict hierarchies.

The many-to-many problem might partially dissolve here too — a concept doesn't need to be in one branch, it just needs to have a height and local neighbors. Participation in multiple relational structures becomes lateral connections at the same height rather than a tree placement problem.

A hypervector is just a very high dimensional binary or real-valued vector, typically thousands of dimensions. The key thing is that at that dimensionality, randomly chosen vectors are almost always nearly orthogonal to each other, which gives you a useful property: you can use them as essentially independent symbols.

The core operations are:

  • Bundling — element-wise addition, produces a vector that's similar to all the inputs. Represents "these things together"
  • Binding — element-wise multiplication (for bipolar) or circular convolution (for complex). Produces a vector dissimilar to both inputs but that can be "unbound" later. Represents association between two things
  • Permutation — shifting elements, used for encoding order or sequence

So you could in principle bind a semantic embedding vector with a scalar-encoded height value, producing a single hypervector that carries both the semantic content and the ontological height, and they're recoverable separately because the binding operation is invertible.

The appeal for your use case is that height becomes a compositional feature rather than a geometric property. You're not reshaping the space, you're bundling an extra dimension of information into the representation in a way that's architecture-agnostic.

The honest caveat is that VSA is most clean when you're working in the hypervector space natively from the start. Retrofitting it onto existing dense embeddings gets messy because those embeddings weren't designed with VSA binding in mind. It might work but it's not the cleanest path.
